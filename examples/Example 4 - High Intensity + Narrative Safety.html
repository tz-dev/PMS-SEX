2) Minimal Vignette

2.1 Scene

Across repeated private or semi-private sexual scenes, a highly intensified arrangement is described by the involved role-positions as safe because the internal story remains coherent: the frame, labels, tone, and shared language continue to explain why the intensity is meaningful, chosen, contained, and repeatable.

The drift does not begin with intensity alone. It begins where the same story becomes easier to reproduce than to re-read, so that after-scene heaviness, reduced stop-capability, reputational or documentation residue, symbolic continuity pressure, uneven repair burden, and the cost of naming costs accumulate outside the story that still calls the arrangement safe.

The scene is not assessed by practice label or by person type. It is assessed by whether narrative safety continues to govern real Ω/Θ costs, Χ distance, Σ integration, and Ψ self-binding, or whether the safety story becomes the mechanism by which those costs are absorbed.

2.2 Temporal boundary

Begins with the repeated high-intensity arrangement and its coherent internal safety story; includes the recurrence by which the same frame, tone, labels, or documentation make repetition easier than correction; ends at the point where after-scene, symbolic, reputational, bodily, relational, or exit costs have accumulated enough to test whether the story still names them or only absorbs them.

2.4 Surface description

A coherent high-intensity arrangement appears safe because its internal grammar remains stable, mutually intelligible, and repeatedly reproducible.

2.5 PMS-SEX question

Does internal narrative coherence remain a real safety structure, or does it become Φ inflation that makes cost naming symbolically expensive, weakens Χ correction, overloads Θ, and strains Σ/Ψ after-scene dignity?

20) Readable Structural Closure

20.1 Summary

This case reads a high-intensity arrangement whose safety claim depends on the coherence of its internal story while external effects accumulate. PMS-SEX does not treat intensity, practice labels, or outer legibility as verdicts; it tests whether □/Φ narration remains connected to Ω/Θ costs, Χ stop-capability, Σ integration, and Ψ self-binding.

The primary drift is narrative substitution: the story can claim safety while after-scene residue, symbolic continuity pressure, documentation or reputational trace, interruption cost, exit cost, exposure, or repair burden remain insufficiently named.

The concrete formula is that narrative safety remains locally intact while repeated high-intensity enactment produces Ω/Θ residue that makes Χ correction socially or symbolically costly, thereby straining Σ/Ψ.

Viability is unstable rather than invalid by default, because a coherent frame may be real, but it becomes structurally strained where cost topology and temporal carryover are absorbed rather than integrated. Dignity-in-Practice is therefore read as strained in the enactment, not as a status claim about any person.
