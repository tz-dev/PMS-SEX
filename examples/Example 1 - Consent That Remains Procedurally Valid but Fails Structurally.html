2) Minimal Vignette

2.1 Scene-bound and non-identifying vignette

A repeated sexual configuration is organized through an explicit consent-based frame. The frame includes articulated rules, declared reversibility, and formal stop-options. No single rupture event occurs. Over time, however, one role experiences the configuration increasingly as cumulative: recovery, emotional processing, bodily after-effects, exposure management, and refusal burden accumulate. The other role experiences the configuration more episodically, with lower downstream handling or greater externalization capacity. The formal consent frame remains legible, but the cost layout becomes increasingly asymmetrical under time. PMS-SEX reads this as a sexual cost-topology problem, not as consent erasure.

  • declared reversibility remains present
  • the phrase “you can always say no” becomes a catch-all stabilizer
  • practical stop-capability becomes less frequently activated
  • cost distribution remains under-articulated because no formal rule has been broken
  • asymmetry-specific responsibility remains implicit rather than explicitly carried

3) Why This Is Repo-Useful

3.1 Structural focus

This example shows why PMS-SEX separates formal frame validity from structural governability. It demonstrates that a scene can remain formally consent-based while still drifting under Α (Attractor) repetition, Ω (Asymmetry) cost asymmetry, Θ (Temporality) accumulation, Χ (Distance) erosion, sexual cost-topology mismatch, and weak asymmetry-specific self-binding.

3.2 Demonstrates

  • consent as frame condition does not erase temporality
  • formal reversibility does not guarantee practical Χ availability
  • no single violation is required for drift to become structurally legible
  • Ω may emerge through after-costs, recovery burden, exposure, and exit friction
  • sexual cost topology may become unequal while consent remains locally intact
  • Ψ relevance may leak into a frame narrated as local or non-binding
  • parity language may become exit from cost re-internalization where unequal burdens accumulate
  • responsibility may drift toward authority if protection, care, or frame maintenance becomes control

3.3 Avoids

  • reducing the case to coercion
  • collapsing consent into pathology
  • treating either role as morally defective
  • using structural cost language as person-ranking
  • treating sexual cost topology as score
  • converting asymmetry-specific responsibility into maturity ranking

9) Adult Sexual Reciprocity and Asymmetry-Specific Responsibility

9.1 Adult sexual reciprocity principle

Adult sexual reciprocity does not require equal burdens. It requires that unequal burdens are not externalized.

9.2 Case application

In this case, reciprocity remains viable only if the role-position with lower downstream handling does not use formal parity to externalize R2’s cumulative cost, and the role-position with higher downstream cost does not use asymmetry as an unmarked standing lever. Both role-positions must carry the self-binding specific to their own cost, power, exposure, exit, and externalization profile.

11.3 Dynamic reading

The frame starts as explicit and procedurally clear. Repetition stabilizes the practice into an attractor. Asymmetry emerges through unequal after-costs and recovery burdens. Time accumulates residues that cannot be reset by renewed consent language alone. Distance becomes harder to activate because no obvious violation has occurred. Eventually, continued participation may begin to carry identity-relevant or relational meaning despite local non-binding framing. Under PMS-SEX_1.2, the drift risk also includes cost-topology mismatch, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, and unmarked asymmetry activation if the unequal cost layout is not re-bound into Σ/Ψ.

20) Readable Structural Closure

Short closure

Consent secures permission. Viability requires cost realism and non-externalization of unequal burdens.

In this configuration, the consent frame does not collapse. It remains explicit, procedurally legible, and locally valid as a frame. The structural problem appears elsewhere: repetition stabilizes the practice into an attractor, asymmetry distributes downstream costs unevenly, and temporality turns local participation into cumulative trajectory.

Once explicit consent becomes the sole stabilizer, Θ and Ω begin to do the coordinating work. What remains formally consensual can still become structurally costly where recovery, refusal, exposure, exit, and interpretive burden accumulate unevenly. The issue is not that consent fails, but that it overperforms: it absorbs pressure that would need to be redistributed into frame re-reading, distance activation, cost-topology naming, or integration.

PMS-SEX_1.2 adds that the relevant adult reciprocity question is not whether burdens are equal, but whether unequal burdens are externalized. Formal parity becomes drift when it exits responsibility under unequal cost topology; asymmetry becomes drift when it is activated as an unmarked standing lever; responsibility becomes drift when it turns into authority rather than self-binding.

The drift is therefore not produced by malice, defective agency, hidden pathology, or failed consent by itself. It is the predictable result of treating a valid consent frame as sufficient while Θ continues to accumulate, Ω remains under-articulated, Χ becomes harder to enact in practice, and Σ/Ψ re-binding does not carry the cost topology.
