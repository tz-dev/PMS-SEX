4) Minimal Vignette

Scene ID: CASE_01_FORMAL_CONSENT_ACCUMULATING_COST

Scene type: repeated consent-based sexual frame under accumulating asymmetric costs

Abstraction level: generic_non_identifying

4.1 Description

A repeated sexual configuration is organized through an explicit consent-based frame. The frame includes articulated rules, declared reversibility, and formal stop-options. No single rupture event occurs. Over time, however, one role experiences the configuration increasingly as cumulative: recovery, emotional processing, bodily after-effects, and refusal burden accumulate. The other role experiences the configuration more episodically, with lower downstream handling. The formal consent frame remains legible, but the cost layout becomes increasingly asymmetrical under time.

4.2 Roles

  • R1: Role with higher initiation frequency and lower downstream exposure after enactment.
    Person status: role_only_not_person_type
  • R2: Role with lower initiation frequency and higher recovery, processing, after-cost, and refusal-handling burden.
    Person status: role_only_not_person_type

4.3 Frame anchor

Operator: □

Explicit consent-based frame with declared rules, articulated stop-options, and the statement that participation remains reversible.

4.4 Temporality

Operator: Θ

Repeated encounters across time. The case is defined by cumulative effects, not by one isolated violation or rupture.

4.5 Trigger

  • Δ (Difference): Difference emerges between formal consent clarity and lived cost distribution.
  • ∇ (Impulse): Pressure rises to maintain continuity and intensity without reopening the frame’s assumptions.

4.6 Λ candidates

Operator: Λ

Expected but missing or underused:

  • pauses that remain non-steering
  • explicit renegotiation after repeated accumulation
  • non-occurrence that does not become guilt, devaluation, or interpretive pressure
  • hesitation treated as structurally meaningful rather than as disruption

4.7 Observed realizations

  • consent is reaffirmed verbally and procedurally
  • declared reversibility remains present
  • the phrase “you can always say no” becomes a catch-all stabilizer
  • practical stop-capability becomes less frequently activated
  • cost distribution remains under-articulated because no formal rule has been broken

5) Why This Is Repo-Useful

5.1 Structural focus

This example shows why PMS-SEX separates formal frame validity from structural governability. It demonstrates that a scene can remain formally consent-based while still drifting under Α repetition, Ω cost asymmetry, Θ accumulation, and Χ erosion.

5.2 Demonstrates

  • consent as frame condition does not erase temporality
  • formal reversibility does not guarantee practical Χ availability
  • no single violation is required for drift to become structurally legible
  • Ω may emerge through after-costs, recovery burden, and exit friction
  • Ψ relevance may leak into a frame narrated as local or non-binding

5.3 Avoids

  • reducing the case to coercion
  • collapsing consent into pathology
  • treating either role as morally defective
  • using structural cost language as person-ranking

9) Drift Trace

Compressed formula:
□ explicit → Α repetition → Ω cost gradient → Θ accumulation → Χ erosion → Ψ leak

Full formula reference:
Δ → ∇ → □ → Λ → Α → Ω → Θ → Φ → Χ → Σ → Ψ

9.1 Dynamic reading

The frame starts as explicit and procedurally clear. Repetition stabilizes the practice into an attractor. Asymmetry emerges through unequal after-costs and recovery burdens. Time accumulates residues that cannot be reset by renewed consent language alone. Distance becomes harder to activate because no obvious violation has occurred. Eventually, continued participation may begin to carry identity-relevant or relational meaning despite local non-binding framing.

9.2 Transitions

  • □ → Α: Explicit consent frame becomes a repeated coordination route.
    Risk: frame stability turns into default continuation.
  • Α → Ω: Repetition reveals unequal recovery, exposure, refusal, and repair burdens.
    Risk: cost gradient remains unnamed because the practice is formally consensual.
  • Ω → Θ: Unequal burdens accumulate over time.
    Risk: exit becomes more expensive without becoming forbidden.
  • Θ → Φ: Narrative reassurance may attempt to reset the scene: “nothing bad is happening because consent remains explicit.”
    Risk: recontextualization replaces integration.
  • Φ → Χ: Distance must become operative to re-read the frame and cost layout.
    Risk: Χ is formally available but practically discouraged.
  • Χ → Σ: Integration would hold both truths: consent may be valid, and the configuration may still be structurally costly.
    Risk: without Σ, the contradiction is displaced.
  • Σ → Ψ: If not handled, continuity begins to carry identity-relevant or relationship-relevant stakes.
    Risk: Ψ leak into a frame narrated as local, procedural, or non-binding.

18) Readable Structural Closure

18.1 Short closure

Consent secures permission. Viability requires cost realism.

18.2 Full closure

In this configuration, the consent frame does not collapse. It remains explicit, procedurally legible, and locally valid as a frame. The structural problem appears elsewhere: repetition stabilizes the practice into an attractor, asymmetry distributes downstream costs unevenly, and temporality turns local participation into cumulative trajectory.

Once explicit consent becomes the sole stabilizer, Θ and Ω begin to do the coordinating work. What remains formally consensual can still become structurally costly where recovery, refusal, exposure, and interpretive burden accumulate unevenly. The issue is not that consent fails, but that it overperforms: it absorbs pressure that would need to be redistributed into frame re-reading, distance activation, cost naming, or integration.

The drift is therefore not produced by malice, defective agency, or hidden pathology. It is the predictable result of treating a valid consent frame as sufficient while Θ continues to accumulate, Ω remains under-articulated, and Χ becomes harder to enact in practice.
