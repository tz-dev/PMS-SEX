1. Purpose and status of this specification

This document specifies the PMS-SEX application profile in a concise, technical, human-readable form. It is based on PMS-SEX.yaml (schema_version = "PMS-SEX_1.1") and renders its identity, constraints, overlay concepts, drift markers, viability criteria, and application discipline explicit for repo, paper, and computational use.

PMS-SEX is a paper-specific application profile of the Praxeological Meta-Structure (PMS). It operationalizes the structural lens of the accompanying paper:

Source paper updated paper reference

PMS-SEX — From Impulse to Self-Binding: A Praxeological Grammar of Sexuality (Δ–Ψ)

The profile is an overlay, not a fork, replacement, or extension of PMS. Its function is to make the sexuality lens formally legible while remaining strictly subordinate to the canonical PMS repository reference specification.

PMS-SEX does not redefine PMS operators, dependencies, derived axes, or meta-axioms. It uses the canonical operator grammar Δ → ∇ → □ → Λ → Α → Ω → Θ → Φ → Χ → Σ → Ψ to read sexual praxis as enacted configuration under frame, non-event, attractor formation, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, integration, and self-binding, with publicness and documentation treated only as application-level □ extensions, never as new operators.

Core idea overlay discipline

PMS-SEX remains valid by staying within what it can responsibly say: not what persons are, but what configurations do over time. It permits hard claims about cost, drift, outer legibility, and consequence without converting those claims into person-status judgments.

4. Validity gate and application firewall

The validity gate constrains PMS application only. PMS itself may be criticized, rejected, revised, or ignored without satisfying this gate. Any use of PMS-SEX as PMS application presupposes:

Χ — Distance Maintained meta-position and stop-capability; no fusion into verdict, urgency, enforcement, person-level attribution, role, impulse, or self-justifying rhetoric. Using PMS-SEX as enforcement, accusation, urgency, or role-fusion.
Reversibility Scene-bound, configuration-specific, revisable readings; no global person labels, irreversible claims, identity verdicts, or ontological attributions. Turning a structural reading into a fixed person-label.
D — Dignity-in-Practice No shaming, humiliation, person-ranking, diagnosis, orientation taxonomy, or person-worth evaluation; critique targets enacted structures and cost handling only. Converting cost, asymmetry, or drift into degraded personhood.

7. Layer separation and misreading guards

Internal framing, outer legibility, public circulation, and temporal carryover may overlap, but must not be collapsed into one another.

7.1 Layer model

Layer Question Misreading prevented
Internal framing How is the scene inhabited or named from within? Assuming internal meaning is false or irrelevant.
Outer legibility What action-form is structurally readable under asymmetry, exposure, repetition, and time? Letting internal labels erase enacted form.
Publicness Does visibility, documentation, circulation, reputation, or audience uptake modify consequences? Treating publicity as either the whole problem or as irrelevant.
Temporal carryover How does the scene continue across Θ? Claiming that later narration rewinds accumulation.

7.2 Guard list

  • Understanding internal meaning does not erase external form.
  • Naming outer legibility does not imply person-ranking, diagnosis, or moral verdict.
  • Publicity may amplify, distribute, or sedimentize readability without necessarily creating it.
  • Practice labels do not by themselves settle outer structural form.
  • Pre-reflective readability does not settle meaning, worth, blame, or consent.
  • Outer legibility does not authorize public accusation by itself.
  • Publicness is an overlay, not the necessary origin of consequence.
  • Internal framing may be real without being total.
Layer discipline non-collapse rule

A scene may be internally framed one way, externally readable another way, publicly circulated in a third way, and temporally carried in a fourth way. PMS-SEX does not collapse these layers.

10. Drift catalogue: core dysfunction markers and corridors

Each marker is a configuration statement: “in this configuration…”. Markers describe predictable structural consequences of operator constellations. They are scene-bound, revisable, and non-diagnostic.

10.1 Core dysfunction markers

(1) Covert self-binding demand (Ψ leak)

Meaning and identity-relevant stakes enter a frame narrated as limited, suspended, local, or non-binding. Typical indicators include implicit expectations, possession logic inside a play frame, meaning claims without explicit frame legibility, and local enactments treated as identity proofs. Structural result: Ψ becomes operative although suspension is asserted.

(2) Asymmetry escalation (Ω escalation)

Asymmetry increases while remaining unacknowledged inside the frame. Typical indicators include boundary testing, access pressure, power shifts without explicit rule updates, uneven refusal or exit capacity, and exposure gradients treated as mutual.

(3) Pseudo-symmetry

Equality rhetoric functions as a lid on cost description. Consent or mutuality language blocks analysis of unequal exposure, after-costs, body remainder, outer legibility, temporality, or publicness overlays.

(4) Non-event escalation (Λ as steering)

Absence becomes an active steering mechanism. Withdrawal, silence, indeterminacy, chronic “maybe,” or unmarked withholding shape practice more than enactment.

(5) Attractor fixation (Α compulsivity)

Stabilization flips into fixation. Practice becomes non-substitutable, repetition replaces choice, alternatives lose practical attractiveness, and practice labels protect the route from re-reading.

(6) Loss of distance (Χ loss)

The meta-layer collapses. Stopping becomes practically unavailable, hesitation is read as betrayal, de-escalation disappears, and outer-form awareness becomes inaccessible.

(7) Temporal overload (Θ accumulation)

Costs accumulate faster than the frame can carry. Exit fictions persist, collapse becomes abrupt, and outer legibility remains after internal closure language claims the scene is over.

(8) Outer-form denial

Internal framing or later recontextualization blocks analysis of enacted form. “It was play,” “service,” “authenticity,” “devotion,” or “consent” is used to end structural reading.

10.2 Dynamic chains

Chain Raw Reading
Primary chain Ψ leak → Ω escalation → Χ loss → Α fixation → Θ overload → exit collapse Once meaning and attribution become covert, Ω becomes a steering variable. If Χ is not operational, the script hardens through Α and temporal costs accumulate until the exit fiction breaks.
Secondary chain internal-label capture → outer-form denial → publicness confusion → delayed cost recognition Practice labels settle the internal story while enacted form remains structurally readable. Public reaction is treated either as the whole problem or as irrelevant.

13. Minimal psychology adapter: structural self-check only

“Minimal psychology” is a name for a structural self-check adapter, not a psychological explanatory layer. It activates where Ch. 18 drift markers and Ch. 19 viability failures are already structurally legible, yet repetition continues unchanged because structural description alone no longer interrupts the path.

13.1 Boundaries

  • no diagnosis
  • no motive theory
  • no person typing
  • no identity ranking
  • no moral evaluation
  • no therapeutic method
  • no conversion into clinical taxonomy

13.2 Activation conditions

The adapter activates only when all of the following hold:

  • drift markers from Chapter 18 are present;
  • minimal viability conditions from Chapter 19 are no longer restoring correction;
  • the configuration is still carried voluntarily and repeatedly.

13.3 Primary structural question

Permitted question structural only

“What structural role is this desire playing in this configuration?”

The colloquial form “Why do I really desire X?” is admissible only if immediately translated back into the structural question above.

13.4 Permitted warning hypothesis

Under sustained drift, one limited hypothesis becomes admissible:

In this configuration, desire-language may be functioning as a drift-maintaining mechanism rather than as play, exploration, negotiated intensity, or bounded practice.

This hypothesis does not explain desire. It is never confirmed psychologically; it is only structurally tested against enacted patterns.

13.5 Test criteria

  • Repeated D-in-Practice failure: enactments repeatedly undermine D as an enactment constraint.
  • Θ damage despite knowledge: foreseeable harm is explicitly acknowledged, but repetition continues unchanged.
  • Progressive frame narrowing: alternatives lose attractiveness or feasibility.
  • Desire used against Χ: desire is invoked to bypass stopping, hesitation, or meta-commentary.
  • Publicness displacement: public reaction is treated as the whole problem while the enacted configuration remains unread.

13.6 Allowed output format

  • “In this configuration, drift persistence is present.”
  • “The configuration is no longer correcting itself structurally.”
  • “Distance (Χ), frame clarity (□), and Θ realism require reactivation.”
  • “Internal framing is no longer sufficient to settle the enacted structure.”
  • “Outer legibility and cost distribution require re-reading without person-ranking.”
Output boundary alarm only

No conclusions about character, pathology, motive, identity, orientation, or worth are permitted.

15. MIP docking interface

20. Analysis checklist: scene-bound application protocol

The analysis checklist is criterial, non-instructional, and non-diagnostic. It must be used with the validity gate: Χ + reversibility + D.

  1. S1 — Scene boundary: Define the scene through □: inside/outside, timing, role grammar, permitted/not permitted, local/binding-relevant. Confirm that all readings remain configuration-bound and revisable.
  2. S2 — Canonical dependency check: Confirm the reading does not alter the PMS v1.3 spine. If using a reduced signature, treat it as shorthand only.
  3. S3 — Reduced operator signature: Select the closest reduced_signatures entry and record chapter reference. Do not infer a new dependency relation from a shorthand cluster.
  4. S4 — Inner / outer / public layer scan: Identify internal framing, outer legibility, publicness, and temporal carryover. Do not let any one layer erase the others.
  5. S5 — Cost axis scan: Identify Ω gradients and Θ trajectory: accumulation, irreversibility, repetition, sedimentation, exit realism.
  6. S6 — Distance and correction capacity: Test whether Χ is practically operative and whether Σ is reachable without coercive simplification or narrative compensation.
  7. S7 — Drift marker scan: Check Ch. 18.3 markers and Ch. 18.4 chains. If Ψ leak is suspected, verify whether binding entered a limited/local/non-binding frame.
  8. S8 — Viability minimums: Apply Ch. 19.3 functional minimums and Ch. 19.4 warning signals. Use the Appendix D corridor as compressed orientation only.
  9. S9 — Path-cost guard: If using disturbance, entropy, path-cost, or stabilization-burden language, confirm that it applies only to configuration dynamics.
  10. S10 — Escalation guard: If drift persists despite structural clarity, the Ch. 20 structural self-check adapter may activate. It outputs alarm only; no motive explanation or diagnosis.
