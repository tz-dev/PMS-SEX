1. Purpose and status of this specification

This document specifies the PMS-SEX application profile in a concise, technical, human-readable form. It is based on PMS-SEX.yaml (schema_version = "PMS-SEX_1.2") and renders its identity, constraints, overlay concepts, reduced signatures, drift markers, sexual cost topology, viability criteria, Dignity-in-Practice discipline, MIP-docking boundary, and application firewall explicit for repo, paper, and computational use.

PMS-SEX is a paper-specific application profile of the Praxeological Meta-Structure (PMS). It operationalizes the structural lens of the accompanying paper:

Source paper updated paper reference

PMS-SEX — From Impulse to Self-Binding: A Praxeological Grammar of Sexuality (Δ–Ψ)

The profile is an overlay, not a fork, replacement, or extension of PMS. Its function is to make the sexuality lens formally legible while remaining strictly subordinate to the canonical PMS repository reference specification.

PMS-SEX does not redefine PMS operators, dependencies, derived axes, or meta-axioms. It uses the canonical operator grammar Δ → ∇ → □ → Λ → Α → Ω → Θ → Φ → Χ → Σ → Ψ to read sexual praxis as enacted configuration under frame, non-event, attractor formation, asymmetry, temporality, recontextualization, distance, integration, and self-binding.

Publicness, outer legibility, pre-reflective readability, path-cost, stabilization burden, body remainder, sexual cost topology, EDEN/PFO asymmetry-switch reference, asymmetry-switch drift, parity-to-exit, responsibility-to-authority, adult sexual reciprocity, and asymmetry-specific responsibility are application-level overlay handles. They are not PMS operators, not new axioms, and not alternative dependency graphs.

Core idea overlay discipline

PMS-SEX remains valid by staying within what it can responsibly say: not what persons are, but what configurations do over time. It permits hard claims about cost, drift, outer legibility, sexual cost topology, D-in-Practice stress, and consequence without converting those claims into person-status judgments, diagnosis, maturity ranking, adultness verdict, or dignity hierarchy.

7. Layer separation and misreading guards

Internal framing, outer legibility, public circulation, and temporal carryover may overlap, but must not be collapsed into one another.

7.1 Layer model

Layer Question Misreading prevented
Internal framing How is the scene inhabited or named from within? Assuming internal meaning is false, irrelevant, or total.
Outer legibility What action-form is structurally readable under asymmetry, exposure, repetition, consequence, and time? Letting internal labels erase enacted form or cost topology.
Publicness Does visibility, documentation, circulation, reputation, or audience uptake modify consequences? Treating publicity as either the whole problem or as irrelevant.
Temporal carryover How does the scene continue across Θ? Claiming that later narration rewinds accumulation.

7.2 Guard list

  • Understanding internal meaning does not erase external form.
  • Naming outer legibility does not imply person-ranking, diagnosis, moral verdict, maturity verdict, or adultness verdict.
  • Publicity may amplify, distribute, document, or sedimentize readability without necessarily creating it.
  • Practice labels do not by themselves settle outer structural form.
  • Pre-reflective readability does not settle meaning, worth, blame, consent, or cost topology.
  • Outer legibility does not authorize public accusation by itself.
  • Publicness is an overlay, not the necessary origin of consequence.
  • Internal framing may be real without being total.
  • Sexual cost topology may be named only as scene-bound cost layout, not as score or person measure.
  • Asymmetry activation must be marked, bounded, and re-bound where it occurs.
  • Parity must not become exit from responsibility.
  • Responsibility must not become authority.
Layer discipline non-collapse rule

A scene may be internally framed one way, externally readable another way, publicly circulated in a third way, and temporally carried in a fourth way. PMS-SEX does not collapse these layers and does not use any one layer to authorize person-ranking.

10. Drift catalogue

Drift markers are configuration statements, not labels. Each marker means: in this configuration... They describe predictable structural consequences of operator constellations; they are scene-bound, revisable, non-diagnostic, and non-person-ranking.

10.1 Core dysfunction markers

Marker Configuration statement Typical indicators Structural result / guard
Covert self-binding demand
Ψ leak		 Meaning and identity-relevant stakes enter a frame narrated as limited, suspended, local, casual, or non-binding. Implicit expectations; jealousy as rule-substitute; meaning claims without explicit frame legibility; local enactments treated as identity proofs; non-binding language denying responsibility already accumulated. Ψ becomes operative although suspension is asserted. Attribution is silently demanded while the frame cannot carry binding explicitly.
Asymmetry escalation
Ω escalation		 Asymmetry increases while remaining unacknowledged inside the frame. Boundary testing; access pressure around time, body, availability, attention, or repair; power shifts without rule updates; uneven refusal, exit, fallout, definitional capacity, exposure, or publicness-related cost. Ω becomes a steering variable rather than a governed structural layout.
Pseudo-symmetry Equality rhetoric functions as a lid on cost description. “We both want it,” “it’s equal,” consent language, mutuality claims, or casual / non-binding language are used to block analysis of unequal exposure, after-costs, or Θ consequences. Non-event escalation
Λ as steering		 Absence becomes an active steering mechanism. Withdrawal as leverage; devaluation through non-occurrence; silence, indeterminacy, “maybe,” “later,” or unmarked withholding inside expectation-bearing frames. Λ shapes practice more than enactment; coordination shifts into interpretation and guessing rather than explicit frame work.
Attractor fixation
Α compulsivity		 Stabilization flips into fixation. Practice becomes non-substitutable; repetition replaces choice; frame breadth shrinks; alternatives lose attractiveness, availability, or recognizability; practice labels protect the route from re-reading. Α binds more strongly than intention.
Loss of distance
Χ loss		 The meta-layer collapses. Stopping becomes practically unavailable; critique or hesitation is read as betrayal, attack, weakness, disloyalty, prudishness, or domination; humor, de-escalation, and outside view disappear. Self-correction fails. Without Χ, drift cannot be intercepted early.
Temporal overload
Θ accumulation		 Costs accumulate faster than the frame can carry. Exit fictions persist; collapse becomes abrupt; publicness documents or sedimentizes already active costs; non-binding language fails to account for bodily, reputational, exposure-related, or binding-related overhang. Exit collapse replaces orderly ending. Θ does not rewind, and Α holds the configuration in place too long for low-cost exit to remain plausible.
Outer-form denial Internal framing or later recontextualization blocks analysis of enacted form. “It was play,” “service,” “authenticity,” “devotion,” “consent,” or “casual” is used to end structural reading; outer legibility is treated as hostile, irrelevant, or merely public misunderstanding. The scene loses its capacity to read itself. Internal meaning may be real, but it does not erase the action-form effective under asymmetry, exposure, repetition, publicness, and time.
Asymmetry-switch drift Asymmetry is activated not as marked emergency or protection under real Ω/Θ overload, but as repeatable leverage, responsibility displacement, or unacknowledged Ψ externalization. Vulnerability, exposure, bodily risk, reputation, or protection is invoked without marking activation as exceptional; parity language remains for access, autonomy, mutuality, or non-binding status; Σ/Ψ re-binding does not occur. Asymmetry no longer reopens coordination; it replaces coordination. It preserves parity language while distributing costs asymmetrically.
Parity-to-exit drift Parity rhetoric is used to deny additional responsibility under unequal sexual cost topology. “It was mutual,” “we are equal,” “no one owes anything,” “the other is autonomous,” or “it was only casual” functions as exit rhetoric. Parity ceases to bind reciprocity and becomes an exit from responsibility. The frame remains rhetorically equal while unequal Ω/Θ costs remain uncoordinated.
Responsibility-to-authority drift Required self-binding under sexual asymmetry is converted into control, frame authority, protection entitlement, or paternalistic governance. “Because I must protect, I may define”; “because asymmetry is real, I may govern the frame”; care becomes control; protection becomes governance; responsibility becomes ownership of the situation. Responsibility stops functioning as Ψ/Χ self-binding and becomes authority. Greater responsibility under asymmetry does not generate a right to control.

13. Minimal Psychology as Structural Self-Check Adapter

Minimal psychology is a restricted structural self-check adapter. It is non-diagnostic, non-interpretive, scene-bound, reversible, and dignity-preserving. It outputs structural alarm only.

13.1 Purpose and boundary

Minimal psychology is not a psychological explanatory layer. It activates where drift markers from Chapter 18 and viability failures from Chapter 19 are already structurally legible, yet repetition continues unchanged because structural description alone no longer interrupts the path.

Boundaries

  • no diagnosis
  • no motive theory
  • no person typing
  • no identity ranking
  • no moral evaluation
  • no therapeutic method
  • no conversion into clinical taxonomy
  • no maturity ranking
  • no person-level assignment of drift markers

If asymmetry-switch drift, parity-to-exit, or responsibility-to-authority is present, minimal psychology still does not explain the pattern psychologically. It asks only whether self-description, desire-language, non-binding language, or protection-language helps the configuration continue despite already visible cost topology.

13.2 Activation conditions

  • Drift markers from Chapter 18 are present.
  • Minimal viability conditions from Chapter 19 no longer restore correction.
  • The configuration is still carried voluntarily and repeatedly.

Typical activation contexts

  • Ψ leak has been named, but repetition continues.
  • Ω costs are legible, but still accepted, switched, denied, or externalized.
  • Θ damage is foreseeable, but stop-capability keeps failing.
  • Χ exists formally, but is not enacted in time.
  • Practice labels continue to block outer-form analysis.
  • Publicness is blamed or ignored while the enacted configuration remains unchanged.
  • Internal self-description becomes more elaborate while the operator chain narrows.
  • Asymmetry activation is named but remains unmarked, unbounded, or unrebound.
  • Parity language repeatedly exits responsibility under unequal cost topology.
  • Responsibility language becomes control, protection entitlement, or frame authority.

13.3 Guiding question

What structural role is this desire playing in this configuration?

The colloquial form “Why do I really desire X?” is admissible only if immediately translated back into the structural question above.

Constraints

  • hypothetical, not confessional
  • targets pattern continuity, not inner truth
  • tests whether desire-language or desire-orientation functions as a drift carrier
  • returns attention to enacted form, cost layout, stop-capability, asymmetry handling, and accumulated consequence
  • may not override outer legibility with a deeper self-story
  • may not explain away switch drift, parity-to-exit, or responsibility-to-authority as personality, trauma, desire, or identity

13.4 Permitted warning hypothesis

In this configuration, desire-language may be functioning as a drift-maintaining mechanism rather than as play, exploration, negotiated intensity, bounded practice, or marked asymmetry activation.

This hypothesis does not explain desire. It is never confirmed psychologically. It is only structurally tested against enacted patterns: repetition, cost concentration, outer-legibility denial, loss of distance, asymmetry escalation, temporal overload, switch drift, parity-to-exit, and responsibility-to-authority.

14.7 Asymmetries on both sides

Asymmetry does not imply one-sidedness. Both sides may face asymmetries, but in different channels.

  • different costs
  • different risks
  • different exposures
  • different exit margins and recovery conditions
  • different capacities to define, refuse, publicize, repair, or absorb consequences
  • different capacities to activate asymmetry
  • different capacities to externalize costs through parity rhetoric
  • different capacities to convert responsibility into authority

Adulthood in PMS-SEX is not maintained by symmetry fictions. It is maintained by explicit asymmetry management within the frame.
